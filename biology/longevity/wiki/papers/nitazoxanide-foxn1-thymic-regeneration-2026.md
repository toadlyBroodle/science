# Nitazoxanide induces FOXN1 and thymic regeneration

## Summary
Genah, Pellegrino, Giansanti et al., Cell Death & Differentiation 2026.
Proteasome inhibition is identified as a previously unrecognized
mechanism that upregulates FOXN1, the master transcription factor of
thymic epithelial cells, in both murine and human primary cells. The
repurposed antiparasitic drug nitazoxanide (NTZ) induced Foxn1 while
preserving cell viability and accelerated functional thymic recovery
after radiation-induced damage.

## Why it matters
Thymic involution drives the decline of naive T-cell output with age, a
core component of immune ageing. A small-molecule, repurposing-ready
route to FOXN1 induction and thymic regeneration is distinct from the
cell-based and reprogramming approaches to
[[topics/immune-rejuvenation]] already in the wiki
([[papers/ipsc-mononuclear-phagocyte-aging-brain-2025]]). NTZ is already
in human use as an antiparasitic, lowering the translational barrier.

## Key findings
- Proteasome inhibition upregulates FOXN1 in murine and human primary
  thymic epithelial cells, a newly described mechanism.
- Nitazoxanide induced Foxn1 expression while preserving cell viability.
- NTZ accelerated functional thymic recovery after radiation damage,
  restoring architecture and cellularity of stromal and hematopoietic
  compartments.
- Preclinical (mouse plus human primary cells); no lifespan or in-human
  endpoint yet.
