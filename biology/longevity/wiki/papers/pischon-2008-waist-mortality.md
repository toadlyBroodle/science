# Waist circumference predicts mortality independently of BMI (EPIC, n=359k)

## Summary
EPIC cohort: **359,387 European adults**, mean follow-up 9.7 years,
14,723 deaths. **Both general adiposity (BMI) and abdominal
adiposity (waist circumference, waist-to-hip ratio) independently
predicted all-cause mortality**. The lowest-risk BMI was 25.3
(men) / 24.3 (women); risk rose at both extremes. Waist
circumference and WHR added prognostic value at every level of BMI.

## Why it matters
Established the case for **measuring waist** alongside BMI in
clinical practice. The "skinny-fat" / normal-BMI / high-WHR
phenotype has the same elevated mortality as obesity per these
data. Underpins modern guidelines that include waist circumference
as a routine vital sign.

## Key findings
- N = 359,387, 9 EU countries, 9.7-yr mean follow-up.
- BMI U-shape: lowest mortality at BMI 25.3 (M) / 24.3 (F).
- Waist circumference: monotonic increase in mortality from
  smallest to largest quintile (HR ~2.0 top vs bottom in men,
  ~1.8 in women).
- WHR: same pattern, slightly stronger than waist alone.
- Effect persisted within every BMI stratum (including normal-BMI
  participants).

## How to act on it
- Measure: tape at level of natural waist (top of iliac crest /
  navel), at end of normal exhale.
- Targets (population-based, not aspirational):
  men <94 cm, women <80 cm; consider intervention above
  102 cm / 88 cm.
- Visceral fat (DEXA, bioimpedance, MRI) is the higher-resolution
  measurement when available.

## Caveats
- Observational. Reverse causation possible (illness causes weight
  loss = elevated mortality at low BMI).
- European cohort; thresholds differ for South Asian, East Asian
  populations.
- Doesn't separate visceral from subcutaneous abdominal fat.
