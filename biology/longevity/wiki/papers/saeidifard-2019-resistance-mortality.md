# Resistance training is independently associated with lower mortality

## Summary
Systematic review and meta-analysis of 11 prospective studies. Adults
who reported any resistance training had **~21% lower all-cause
mortality** vs. those with none, after adjusting for aerobic
exercise. Effect was **additive when combined with aerobic exercise
(combined ~40% lower mortality)**, suggesting the two modalities
work through distinct mechanisms.

## Why it matters
Closes the loop from "grip strength predicts mortality"
([[papers/leong-2015-pure-grip-strength]]) to "training to raise
strength reduces mortality." This is the **interventional**
evidence behind the strength-training recommendation, not just an
observational biomarker association.

## Key findings
- 11 prospective cohorts pooled.
- Resistance training (any reported amount) vs. none: ~21% lower
  all-cause mortality.
- Resistance training + aerobic exercise: ~40% lower mortality vs.
  neither (additive effect).
- Resistance training alone: significant mortality reduction even in
  participants meeting aerobic exercise guidelines.
- Cardiovascular and cancer mortality both reduced.

## How to act on it
- 2 full-body resistance sessions per week, progressive load on
  compound movements (squat, hinge, press, pull, carry).
- 30-45 min per session is the sweet spot.
- Free weights or machines both work; novelty matters less than
  consistent progressive overload.

## Caveats
- Observational, not RCT.
- Self-reported resistance training; intensity / volume not
  standardized.
- Strongest effect comes from "any vs none" comparison; dose-response
  curve above some minimum is less clear.
