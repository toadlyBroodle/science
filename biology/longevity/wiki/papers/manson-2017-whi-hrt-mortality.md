# WHI 18-year follow-up: HRT mortality-neutral overall

## Summary
Manson et al. cumulative 18-year follow-up (intervention + extended
post-trial) of the **Women's Health Initiative** hormone therapy
RCTs: **n=27,347 postmenopausal women age 50–79** randomised in
1993–1998 to conjugated equine estrogen + medroxyprogesterone
(CEE+MPA) or estrogen alone (CEE; women with prior hysterectomy)
or placebo. Mortality follow-up available for >98% of participants;
7,489 deaths analyzed.

**All-cause mortality:** 27.1% HRT vs 27.6% placebo,
**HR 0.99 (95% CI 0.94–1.03)** in the pooled cohort.
- CEE + MPA arm: HR 1.02 (0.96–1.08).
- CEE-alone arm: HR 0.94 (0.88–1.01).

Neutral on all-cause and cause-specific mortality overall.

## Why it matters
Definitive long-term mortality follow-up of the trials whose initial
2002–2004 readouts drove a worldwide collapse in HRT prescribing on
the basis of relative-risk increases in breast cancer and
cardiovascular events that were largely confined to older,
late-initiation users. The 2017 follow-up shows **no excess
all-cause mortality** even with 18 years of follow-up. For longevity
purposes the relevant question is no longer "is HRT lethal?" — it's
not — but rather where the timing-hypothesis benefit lies for
younger, recently-menopausal women.

## Key findings
- All-cause mortality HR 0.99 (CI 0.94–1.03).
- CV mortality, cancer mortality, and other-cause mortality each
  not significantly different from placebo.
- **Age-stratified:** HRs were nominally lower for women
  initiating at age 50–59 vs 70–79, but the all-cause-mortality
  trend did not reach significance. The early-initiation subgroup
  is consistent with the timing hypothesis but does not prove it.
- Breast cancer incidence diverged by arm (slight increase with
  CEE+MPA, slight decrease with CEE alone) without translating
  into mortality differences over 18 years.

## Caveats
- WHI used CEE and CEE+MPA. Modern menopause practice often uses
  transdermal 17β-estradiol + micronised progesterone, which has
  a more favourable risk profile (lower thromboembolic risk; the
  KEEPS and ELITE trials are the relevant follow-ups). Results
  here are specific to oral CEE-based formulations.
- Average age at randomisation was 63 — older than the typical
  modern HRT initiator. Most current prescribing is for women in
  their 50s near or shortly after menopause, where the WHI evidence
  is thinner but the mechanistic case is stronger.
- The trial's 2002 early termination on relative-risk grounds
  remains a textbook case of the absolute-vs-relative-risk
  communication failure: the absolute increase in adverse events
  was small, but the relative-risk framing dominated the public
  narrative. The Reddit-thread framing of the post around statin
  underprescribing follows a similar pattern.

## Practical takeaway
- For women in or near the menopause transition with bothersome
  vasomotor or genitourinary symptoms, modern HRT (transdermal
  estradiol + micronised progesterone) carries a modest
  risk-benefit profile that is not the cardiovascular catastrophe
  the WHI 2002 readout was misinterpreted to imply.
- For longevity-without-symptoms framing in women without
  indications, RCT-level evidence does not currently support
  starting HRT.
- Decisions are highly individualised and should account for
  symptom burden, breast-cancer history, thromboembolic risk
  factors, and timing relative to menopause.
