# The U-shaped sleep-mortality curve, ~1.4M adults

## Summary
Cappuccio et al. systematic review and meta-analysis of **16
prospective studies, ~1.4 million participants**, follow-up >3 years.
Both **short sleep (<6 hr)** and **long sleep (>9 hr)** were
associated with elevated all-cause mortality vs. the 7-8 hr
reference: **short-sleep RR 1.12, long-sleep RR 1.30**. The
classical U-shaped sleep-duration mortality curve.

## Why it matters
Most-cited primary source for the "7-9 hours per night" sleep
recommendation. Establishes that sleep duration is not just a
correlate of mortality but a dose-dependent one. Note: **the
long-sleep arm is more likely to reflect underlying illness as a
cause** (sicker people sleep more) than long sleep itself causing
death. Causal interpretation is cleaner for short sleep.

## Key findings
- 16 prospective cohorts, ~1.4M adults.
- Short sleep (<6 hr/night): RR 1.12 (95% CI 1.06-1.18).
- Long sleep (>9 hr/night): RR 1.30 (95% CI 1.22-1.38).
- Reference: 7-8 hr.
- Effect persists across age, sex, and country.

## How to act on it
- 7-9 hours per night is the target.
- Fixed wake time is the highest-leverage lever (recent work
  suggests **regularity** may matter more than duration; see
  Windred et al. 2024 SLEEP).
- Treat treatable causes of short sleep (sleep apnea, restless
  legs, anxiety) before optimizing duration.

## Caveats
- Observational; confounding by health status is substantial,
  particularly for long sleep.
- Self-reported sleep duration; objective (PSG / actigraphy) data
  was sparse in 2010.
- "U-shape" is somewhat artifact of the analysis; a flat valley
  at 6.5-8.5 hr is more accurate than a single 7-hr point optimum.
