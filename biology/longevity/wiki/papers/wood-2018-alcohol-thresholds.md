# Lowest-risk alcohol intake is ~100 g/week (~7 standard drinks)

## Summary
IPD pooled analysis of **599,912 current drinkers** (no prior CVD)
from **83 prospective studies in 19 high-income countries**. Lowest
**all-cause mortality** observed at **~100 g/week of pure alcohol
(~7 UK units, ~5-7 US standard drinks)**. Above that, all-cause
mortality and stroke risk rose monotonically. **For most CVD
subtypes (especially stroke and fatal hypertensive disease), there
was no safe lower threshold**: less is better.

## Why it matters
The clearest modern evidence base for the "if you drink, less is
better" framing that supplanted the older "moderate drinking is
protective" narrative. Note: this study **only included current
drinkers** (excluded never-drinkers and former drinkers), addressing
the "sick quitter" confound that inflated the apparent benefit of
moderate drinking in older studies.

## Key findings
- N = 599,912 drinkers without prior CVD.
- Lowest all-cause mortality at ~100 g/week pure ethanol.
- Above 100 g/week: each 100 g/week increment associated with a
  ~6 month reduction in life expectancy at age 40.
- For stroke, fatal hypertensive disease, and aortic aneurysm: no
  safe threshold; risk rose continuously from zero.
- For non-fatal MI: a small inverse association (the "wine
  protective" signal) survived but was modest.

## How to act on it
- If you don't drink, don't start.
- If you do, ≤7 standard drinks per week (men or women) is the
  evidence-supported upper bound.
- The lower-still GBD 2018 framing ("no safe level") is consistent
  with this paper for CVD subtypes other than non-fatal MI.

## Caveats
- Excludes never-drinkers, so doesn't directly test "no alcohol vs
  some alcohol."
- Observational. Residual confounding by socioeconomic status and
  health-conscious behaviour likely.
- High-income countries only.
