# VITAL: vitamin D supplementation does not reduce mortality (negative RCT)

## Summary
**VITAL** is a 2x2 factorial RCT of **vitamin D3 (2000 IU/day) +
marine omega-3 (1 g/day)** vs placebo in **25,871 US adults** (men
≥50, women ≥55), median **5.3-year follow-up**. Vitamin D **did not
significantly reduce** the primary endpoints: invasive cancer
(HR 0.96), major CVD events (HR 0.97), or **all-cause mortality
(HR 0.99)**. A modest signal toward reduced **cancer mortality**
emerged after excluding the first 1-2 years (HR 0.75-0.79). Omega-3
results published separately and were similarly null on most
primary endpoints.

## Why it matters
Largest, longest, best-powered RCT of vitamin D supplementation for
cancer / CVD / mortality. Cleanly **disconfirms** the "vitamin D
deficiency causes cardiovascular and cancer mortality" hypothesis
that drove a decade of supplementation enthusiasm based on
observational data. Should anchor any recommendation about
universal vitamin D supplementation: **for non-deficient adults,
the evidence is null**.

## Key findings
- N = 25,871, median 5.3 yr follow-up.
- Vitamin D 2000 IU/day vs placebo:
  - Invasive cancer: HR 0.96 (95% CI 0.88-1.06), null.
  - Major CVD events: HR 0.97 (95% CI 0.85-1.12), null.
  - All-cause mortality: HR 0.99 (95% CI 0.87-1.12), null.
- Cancer mortality with latency exclusions: HR 0.75-0.79
  (suggestive but not pre-specified primary).
- Subgroup with normal BMI: cancer-incidence HR 0.76 (significant);
  obese subgroup: no benefit.
- No significant safety signal.

## How to act on it
- For non-deficient adults: routine vitamin D supplementation is
  not supported for mortality / CVD / cancer prevention.
- Test 25-OH vitamin D once; supplement only if deficient (<20
  ng/mL) or borderline with risk factors.
- Target serum level (if supplementing) is 30-50 ng/mL; megadosing
  is not supported and can be harmful.
- The cancer-mortality latency signal is interesting but
  hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.

## Caveats
- US-only cohort with relatively high baseline 25-OH vitamin D;
  results may differ in deficient populations.
- 2000 IU/day is a relatively low dose; some argue higher doses
  needed for effect.
- Co-intervention with omega-3 may interact (factorial design).
