# Smoking shortens life by 10 years; quitting recovers most of it

## Summary
Jha et al. analysed mortality among **113,752 women and 88,496 men**
≥25 years old, interviewed 1997-2004, in the US National Health
Interview Survey linked to mortality follow-up. Current smokers had
**~3x the all-cause mortality rate** of never-smokers (HR 3.0 women,
2.8 men). Life expectancy was shortened by **>10 years** in current
smokers. Quitting at age **25-34, 35-44, or 45-54 recovered ~10, ~9,
or ~6 years** of life respectively.

## Why it matters
Single most-cited modern primary source for the magnitude of
smoking's harm. The 10-year life-expectancy loss is the canonical
"this is bigger than any drug intervention" comparison. The age-of-
quitting gradient gives the central reason that cessation is worth
doing at any age but worth more the earlier.

## Key findings
- Modern (post-1980) US cohort: smoking mortality ratio ~3 vs
  never-smokers (a doubling vs the older Doll & Peto data).
- 10+ year life-expectancy loss from continued smoking.
- Quitting before 40 avoids most of the excess mortality.
- Pattern symmetric across men and women.

## How to act on it
- Don't start. If you smoke: quit, the earlier the better.
- The reduction in mortality begins within months and is largely
  complete within ~10 years of quitting.
- Combination pharmacotherapy (varenicline + NRT) plus behavioural
  support has the highest cessation rates.

## Caveats
- Observational, but the effect size is so large that residual
  confounding can't plausibly explain it.
- Doesn't separate by intensity of smoking; pack-years matter.
- US cohort; absolute mortality patterns vary internationally.
