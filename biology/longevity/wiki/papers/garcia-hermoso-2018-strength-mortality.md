# Muscular strength and mortality, pooled across ~2M adults

## Summary
Systematic review and meta-analysis pooling **~2 million adults** from
38 prospective cohorts. Higher levels of **upper-body** (handgrip)
**and lower-body** (chair-rise, knee extension) muscular strength
were inversely associated with all-cause mortality, **regardless of
age, sex, or follow-up period**. Effect held after adjustment for
cardiorespiratory fitness in the subset of studies that measured it.

## Why it matters
Largest pooled estimate of the strength-mortality association
available. Establishes that **strength is independently
load-bearing** even after accounting for aerobic fitness, and that
the relationship is not specific to grip strength alone (a possible
confounder in [[papers/leong-2015-pure-grip-strength]]).

## Key findings
- 38 prospective studies, ~2 million adults pooled.
- Highest vs lowest muscular strength tertile: HR ~0.75 for
  all-cause mortality.
- Effect across both upper-body and lower-body strength measures.
- Persists after adjustment for CRF where measured.
- Holds across age groups (including >65) and across follow-up
  windows (5-25 years).

## Caveats
- Observational. Strength may proxy for total muscle mass and
  protein adequacy.
- Heterogeneous strength measures across cohorts (grip, chair-rise,
  knee extension).
- Study quality varied; sensitivity analyses excluding
  lower-quality studies preserve the effect.
