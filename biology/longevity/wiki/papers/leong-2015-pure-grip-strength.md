# Grip strength predicts mortality more than systolic BP (PURE, n=139,691)

## Summary
PURE study analysis of **139,691 adults across 17 countries**, ages
35-70, followed median 4 years. Grip strength was inversely
associated with **all-cause mortality (HR 1.16 per 5 kg lower
grip)**, cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.17), non-cardiovascular
mortality (HR 1.17), MI (HR 1.07), and stroke (HR 1.09). **Grip
strength was a stronger predictor of all-cause and cardiovascular
mortality than systolic blood pressure** in the same cohort.

## Why it matters
Cheapest, most reproducible single biomarker of "global aging" in
clinical use. A $30 hand dynamometer beats a sphygmomanometer for
mortality prediction. Cited as the canonical evidence that
**muscle strength is independently load-bearing for healthspan**,
not just a downstream effect of cardiorespiratory fitness.

## Key findings
- N = 139,691 adults across 17 high-, middle-, and low-income
  countries.
- HR per 5 kg reduction in grip strength:
  - All-cause mortality: 1.16 (95% CI 1.13-1.20).
  - CV mortality: 1.17.
  - Non-CV mortality: 1.17.
  - MI: 1.07.
  - Stroke: 1.09.
- Effect persists after adjustment for age, sex, education,
  employment status, physical activity, alcohol, tobacco, and
  diet.
- Grip strength predicted CV mortality more strongly than SBP did.

## How to act on it
- Measure: cheap hand dynamometer, max of 3 trials per hand,
  best-hand value.
- Targets (rough, by age 40-49): male >45 kg, female >27 kg.
- Train: any compound resistance training raises grip.
  See [[papers/saeidifard-2019-resistance-mortality]] for the
  mortality benefit of resistance training itself.

## Caveats
- Observational. Grip strength may proxy for total muscle mass,
  general health status, or socioeconomic factors not fully
  captured.
- Low- and middle-income subjects had different baseline grip
  norms; absolute thresholds are population-dependent.
