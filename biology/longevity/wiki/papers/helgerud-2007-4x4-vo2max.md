# The 4x4 protocol that raises VO2max ~7% in 8 weeks

## Summary
Helgerud, Hoydal, Wang et al. compared four training regimes
(15s/15s intervals, **4 min × 4 intervals at 90-95% HRmax**, lactate
threshold continuous, long slow distance) on VO2max gains over 8
weeks in moderately trained subjects. Both interval protocols beat
moderate-intensity continuous training. The **4x4 group gained
7.2% VO2max** (55.5 → 60.4 mL/kg/min); the 15/15 group gained 5.5%.
The lactate-threshold and long-slow-distance groups did not improve
VO2max significantly. Stroke volume of the heart increased
significantly more in the 4x4 group.

## Why it matters
The single most-cited primary source for the **Norwegian 4x4
protocol** that VO2max-raising programs are built on. Demonstrates
that the limiting factor on VO2max gain is **time spent near max
heart rate**, and 4-minute intervals are the practical optimum.

## The protocol
- Warm up 10 min at ~70% HRmax.
- 4 minutes at **90-95% HRmax** ("can only get a few words out").
- 3 minutes active recovery at ~70% HRmax.
- Repeat 4 times total.
- Cool down 5 min.
- Frequency: 2-3 sessions per week for 8 weeks.

## Key findings
- 4x4 group: **+7.2% VO2max** in 8 weeks (55.5 → 60.4 mL/kg/min).
- 15/15 group: **+5.5% VO2max**, similar VO2max gain but smaller
  stroke-volume gain.
- Lactate threshold and long-slow-distance groups: no significant
  VO2max change.
- Time near VO2max (above 90% HRmax) was the most predictive
  variable.

## Caveats
- N is small (~40 subjects) by modern standards.
- Subjects were already moderately trained males. Untrained
  beginners typically see larger relative gains (10-15% in
  follow-up studies); elite athletes typically see <5%.
- The widely-circulated "+13% in 8 weeks" claim is not from this
  paper. It comes from secondary summaries that conflated this
  study with later trials in untrained or clinical populations.
- Older study (2007); has been replicated many times since.
