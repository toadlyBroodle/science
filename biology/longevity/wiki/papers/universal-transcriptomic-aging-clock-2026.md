# Cross-mammal transcriptomic clocks of ageing and mortality

## Summary
Tyshkovskiy et al. (Gladstone/Harvard, Mass General Brigham), Nature
2026. Integrating over 11,000 transcriptomes spanning more than 25
tissues and 4 mammals (mouse, rat, macaque, human), the authors build
interpretable chronological-age and mortality clocks and identify
conserved universal ageing and mortality genes that change almost
identically across species.

## Why it matters
Adds a conserved transcriptomic axis to the wiki's clock coverage,
which until now centred on methylation, proteomic, and single-cell
clocks ([[papers/nc-2025-14clocks]], [[papers/organ-proteomic-clocks-2025]],
[[papers/scageclock-2026]]). The cross-species conservation argues the
signatures are causal hallmarks rather than species-specific
correlates, and the mortality clock provides a transferable readout for
scoring rejuvenation interventions across model organisms.

## Key findings
- Conserved universal ageing/mortality genes (GPNMB, CDKN1A, LGALS3)
  change near-identically across mouse, rat, macaque, and human.
- Separate clocks for distinct cellular modules (inflammation, energy
  production, DNA repair) show ageing is multi-process, not unitary.
- The mortality clock predicts proximity to death and detects
  rejuvenation signatures across species.
- Built on >11,000 transcriptomes, >25 tissues, 4 mammalian species.
