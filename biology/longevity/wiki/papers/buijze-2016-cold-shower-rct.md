# Cold-shower RCT: 29% reduction in self-reported sickness absence

## Summary
Buijze et al. PLOS ONE pragmatic RCT: **n=3,018 adults age 18–65
without severe comorbidity**, randomised to a daily cold shower
(30, 60, or 90 seconds) or no cold shower, for **30 consecutive
days** plus an optional 60-day continuation. Primary outcome:
self-reported sickness absence from work and number of illness
days.

**Cold-shower groups (pooled) vs control: 29% reduction in
self-reported sickness absence** (incident-rate ratio 0.71,
p=0.003). **No significant change in number of illness days
themselves** — i.e., people felt sick about as often, but missed
work less.

No dose-response across 30/60/90-second arms. Quality-of-life,
work-productivity, anxiety, and thermal-sensation outcomes were
either neutral or modestly favorable for the cold groups.

## Why it matters
Largest RCT to date on routine cold exposure. Effect size on the
sickness-absence proxy is sizeable, but the endpoint is soft — a
self-reported behavioural outcome rather than a biomarker or hard
clinical event. The dissociation between "fewer absences" and
"same number of illness days" suggests the mechanism may be
**psychological / activation-based** rather than immunological.

## Key findings
- Sickness-absence IRR 0.71 (p=0.003).
- No effect on illness-days count.
- No dose-response across 30/60/90 s.
- ~64% of cold-group participants continued cold showering
  voluntarily after the 30-day mandatory period — adherence in
  practice is non-trivial.

## Caveats
- **Soft endpoint.** Self-reported sickness absence, not
  mortality, MACE, or biomarker-validated outcome.
- **Unblinded.** Cold exposure is unblindable; expectancy /
  placebo effects are plausible contributors.
- **Population.** Generally healthy adults; effects in
  immunocompromised, elderly, or cardiac patients unknown.
- **No mortality / hard cardiovascular evidence** for cold
  exposure exists at RCT scale. Brown-fat activation, cold-shock
  protein induction, and metabolic effects are
  mechanism-supported but not endpoint-validated.
- Cold exposure has acute risks: vasoconstriction stress on
  unrecognised cardiovascular disease, cold-induced arrhythmia
  in vulnerable individuals.

## Practical takeaway
- Cold exposure is a soft intervention with one good-sized RCT
  on a soft endpoint and a plausible but not endpoint-validated
  mechanism.
- 30 seconds at the end of a regular shower is the simplest
  protocol with positive evidence; longer durations did not
  produce additional benefit in this trial.
- Don't position cold showers as a longevity intervention; the
  evidence is at the "interesting wellness practice" level, not
  the mortality-RCT level.
- If you have known cardiovascular disease, discuss with a
  physician before adopting habitual cold exposure.
